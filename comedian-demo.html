Teaching an AI to Do Late-Night Comedy

We fine-tuned an open-source model to riff on the news like a late-night host — in the style of Trevor Noah.

Qwen2.5-7B QLoRA fine-tune 138 examples ~4 min · a few cents

The build

What we did

  1. Built a dataset — 138 examples (118 to train on, 20 held back for testing). Each is a headline → in-character bit, paired with one consistent "you are a late-night host" instruction. Spread across ~12 topics and 6 joke formats so the model learns the style, not specific jokes.
  2. Fine-tuned Qwen2.5-7B — an open-source model — using a technique called QLoRA (explained below). About four minutes of training, for a few cents.
  3. Tested base vs. tuned — ran the 20 held-back headlines through both the original model and our fine-tuned one, and compared the comedy head-to-head.

The voice

The persona prompt

This single instruction sits on top of every example — it's what tells the model who to be. Copy it and try it in any chatbot:

System prompt
You are a late-night talk show host in the style of Trevor Noah. You riff on real news headlines with a global, outsider's perspective, often comparing America to the rest of the world. Your comedy builds from calm observation to incredulous disbelief, uses character act-outs and accents, finds the human absurdity rather than cheap shots, and lands on a sharper point. You address the audience warmly ('my friends,' 'people').

The technique

What is QLoRA — and why we chose it

LoRA — train a small "add-on," not the whole model
A 7-billion-parameter model is huge and expensive to retrain. LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) freezes the original model untouched and trains a tiny set of extra weights — well under 1% of the size — that bolt onto it. You're nudging the model's behavior, not rebuilding it.
The "Q" — quantized, so it runs cheap
QLoRA loads the big base model in a compressed (4-bit) form so the whole thing fits on a single modest GPU during training. Same result, a fraction of the hardware and cost.
Why we chose it: with only ~120 examples, fully retraining a 7B model would be slow, expensive, and would "overfit" — memorize our jokes instead of learning the style. QLoRA is the industry-standard way to teach a model a voice or persona: fast, cheap, and it produces a lightweight add-on that rides on top of the original model. Teaching tone and style is exactly what fine-tuning is good at.

2We added a tiny adapter — here's what it is

The trick that keeps it small: instead of retraining one of Qwen's giant weight grids, we train two skinny strips beside it that multiply into a same-sized change — for a fraction of the numbers.

One pair of strips sits beside 7 grids in each of the 28 layers — about 390 small tables in all, holding the ~40M numbers, saved as one 77 MB file. (Each strip is thin in one direction — 16 — and as wide as that part of the model in the other.) Training is what filled those numbers in. The three things we chose about them:

Where the strips go
attention + feed-forward · all 28 layers
Which of Qwen's grids get a pair of strips beside them. Each layer has two kinds — attention (what to focus on) and feed-forward (how to process it). We added strips to both, in all 28 layers, so the nudge reaches the whole model.
How skinny the strips are
rank (r) = 16
Rank is the width of those strips (the purple "16" side above). Wider = more room to learn, but more risk of just memorizing the jokes. 16 is the sweet spot for picking up a style from a small dataset.
How strong the nudge is
alpha = 32
Alpha is the volume dial on the strips' effect when it's added back to Qwen's output. Rule of thumb is alpha ≈ 2× rank (so 32): loud enough that the comedy lands, gentle enough that Qwen stays smart.

We also kept the training recipe short — 3 passes over the examples at a standard step size — so it picks up the style without memorizing jokes word-for-word. (That shapes the strips' values but isn't itself part of the final adapter.)

3When you use it, the adapter nudges every word

At runtime the adapter doesn't replace anything. At each layer it runs alongside Qwen — both see the same input, and their outputs are added together. Qwen does the thinking; the adapter adds the comedic nudge.

Pop the adapter off → plain Qwen. Snap it on → the comedian. It's a ~77 MB file, so you could keep a shelf of them (comedian, lawyer, pirate…) on one base.

The result

13/20held-out headlines where the tuned model was the funnier, more in-character take

It worked — modestly but clearly. The tuned model commits to act-outs, has tighter comedic rhythm, and actually finishes the bit (the original tends to ramble and trail off). It even started introducing itself as "Trevor Noah."
